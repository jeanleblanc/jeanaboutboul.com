## Fourier Series

Any periodic function $f(x)$ with period $2\pi$ can be decomposed into a sum
of sines and cosines:

$$
f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_n \cos(nx) + b_n \sin(nx) \right]
$$

where the Fourier coefficients are given by:

$$
a_n = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x) \cos(nx) \, dx, \qquad
b_n = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x) \sin(nx) \, dx
$$

## Convergence

### Pointwise Convergence

If $f$ is piecewise smooth, the Fourier series converges pointwise to $f(x)$
at every point of continuity, and to the average of left and right limits at
jump discontinuities.

### The Gibbs Phenomenon

Near discontinuities, partial sums overshoot by approximately $9\%$ of the
jump, regardless of the number of terms. This is the *Gibbs phenomenon*.

## The Fourier Transform

For non-periodic functions, we pass to the Fourier transform:

$$
\hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, e^{-2\pi i \xi x} \, dx
$$

with the inversion formula:

$$
f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) \, e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi
$$

## Applications

### Solving the Heat Equation

The heat equation $u_t = \alpha u_{xx}$ on $\mathbb{R}$ with initial condition
$u(x,0) = f(x)$ has the explicit solution:

$$
u(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\alpha t}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(y) \, e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4\alpha t}} \, dy
$$

This is obtained by taking the Fourier transform in $x$ and solving the resulting ODE.
